	•	Project Q&A’s (for internal use in Phase 1) – Dec 25 to Feb 26

The Project Q&As will be a living document – questions will be added and refined as engagement continues.

Q. Who is AET?
AET (Advanced Energy Tech) is a locally based, regionally focused company that turns waste into valuable resources using modern, AI-enabled sorting and recovery technology.
Our team draws on decades of experience in engineering, construction, agriculture and regional infrastructure delivery. This gives us a practical, grounded understanding of the waste and recycling challenges faced by regional communities – and how to solve them.
We are long-term contributors to the Loddon Mallee region, with deep local relationships and a commitment to supporting local jobs, industry and innovation. Our work is focused on delivering real environmental, economic and community benefits for future generations, and helping regional Victoria build a stronger, more resilient circular economy.
Q. What does AET do?
AET develops smart projects that turn everyday waste into valuable resources – reducing landfill, cutting emissions and keeping value in the region.
 We help regional communities move toward a circular economy by designing systems that recover materials, support local industry and create long-term environmental benefits.
 AET’s Pyramid Hill R&D facility plays a critical role in this work. It allows the team to test how organic waste can be transformed into valuable carbon products and, by working with local businesses, identify practical ways to return these materials to industry. These proven methods will be applied at the Advanced Resource Recovery Centre (ARRC) to ensure as much waste as possible is put to good use.

Q. What is the ARRC project?
The ARRC is a clean, fully enclosed, modern facility that will take everyday waste from Bendigo and surrounding regions and turns it into useful materials for local industry.

The facility uses AI, robotics and precision sorting to recover valuable materials from mixed red-bin and yellow-bin waste. Its flexible, modular design allows it to handle the regional challenges of varying waste volumes, mixed materials and changing input quality over time.

The ARRC also incorporates the latest waste treatment technology, including cleaning, separating and sorting waste into organics and mixed recyclables. By removing organics from the waste stream, the facility will cut methane emissions and significantly reduce the region’s greenhouse-gas footprint.




Q. What stage is the project at?

AET has completed the early feasibility and development work for the ARRC and is now working closely with the City of Greater Bendigo through the next stages of the project’s assessment and approval process. 

Current work is focused on finalising project development, preparing for detailed engineering, and ensuring key stakeholders have a clear, shared understanding before the project moves into detailed design and delivery.

Q. What technology will the ARRC use?

The ARRC will use proven, globally deployed sorting and resource-recovery technologies. The exact configuration will be finalised through detailed design to ensure the highest performance and environmental outcomes for Bendigo.

Q. Will AI reduce the number of local jobs at the ARRC?

No. AI is used to improve safety, accuracy and recycling outcomes, but the facility will rely on a skilled local workforce. 

AI does the repetitive and high-precision sorting work, allowing people to focus on the roles that require judgement, problem-solving and technical skills.
 The ARRC will create a wide range of ongoing roles, including operations, maintenance, circular-economy product development, education programs and partnerships with local businesses. 
AI enhances the work – it doesn’t remove the need for people.

Q. What benefits will this project have for the local community?

The ARRC will deliver clear, practical and long-term benefits, including:

	•	hundreds of local jobs during construction and up to 150 ongoing roles
	•	more local industry and stronger regional supply chains
	•	fewer waste trucks travelling long distances
	•	reduced landfill and lower greenhouse gas emissions
	•	cleaner neighbourhoods and better recycling outcomes
	•	keeping economic value and skills in the region

It turns everyday waste into new products made by local businesses – keeping value local.

Q. Will the project/construction of the facility have any negative impacts on the community?

The facility will be carefully managed to minimise disruption and impacts on the community. It will be:

	•	fully enclosed
	•	designed to minimise noise, odour, traffic and visual impacts
	•	regulated under strict EPA requirements
	•	supported by ongoing monitoring and transparent reporting

The community will be kept informed as the project progresses and will be notified well in advance of any planned works. A Community Reference Group will also be established to ensure local voices are heard and to support genuine two-way engagement at every stage of the project.

Q. Why should my council get on board?

The ARRC gives councils a stable, future-ready and cost-effective way to manage waste.

Right now, the Loddon Mallee generates over 700,000 tonnes of waste across all streams – household waste (red, yellow and green bins), commercial and industrial waste, and construction waste. While the region has some existing infrastructure, including a materials recovery facility (MRF) in Echuca for yellow-bin recycling, there is no facility in the region that can sort and recover materials from mixed household waste or create integrated circular pathways like the ARRC. 

Because of this gap, valuable resources continue to leave the region, taking jobs, investment and opportunity with them.

The ARRC changes that by: 
	•	providing predictable long-term service pricing
	•	helping meet state and national recovery targets
	•	supporting local jobs and circular industries
	•	reducing reliance on metropolitan facilities
	•	cutting transport distances and emissions
	•	positioning the region as a leader in modern resource recovery

This is not just a waste solution – it’s a long-term regional economic and environmental strategy.

Q. How can my business get involved?

The ARRC will open up new opportunities for local businesses by supplying consistent, high-quality recovered materials.

This innovative project will create a new local circular economy- one that relies on local skills, services, and innovation to thrive.

Businesses can get involved by:
	•	providing services and supplies during design, construction and operations
	•	partnering on new circular products and local manufacturing
	•	participating in industry engagement sessions
	•	joining the Community Reference Group
	•	trialling innovation projects using recovered materials
	•	developing new product lines supported by reliable local feedstock

This project will support a new regional circular economy – one that creates jobs and opportunities here, not in metropolitan areas.

Q. How can schools and universities get involved?

Education opportunities will be developed as the project progresses, including:
	•	STEM and circular economy programs
	•	school visits
	•	curriculum materials
	•	site tours (when appropriate)
	•	youth engagement through the Youth Councils

There is strong demand from students and teachers for real-world sustainability learning, and the ARRC will support this.

Q. How will the community be kept updated?

AET is committed to keeping the community informed and involved at every stage of the project.

Community updates will be provided through:
	•	A Community Reference Group – providing a forum for local ideas, questions and feedback, ensuring the community can help shape project outcomes.
	•	Regular updates and engagement opportunities – including information sessions, newsletters, and briefings – keeping residents informed as the project progresses.
	•	Clear governance and communication channels – ensuring key information is shared consistently and openly with all stakeholders.
	•	Close partnership with councils – providing another trusted avenue for local updates and community conversations.
	•	Proactive media engagement – supporting transparency and help keep the broader region informed.

Q. Is this waste-to-energy?
No. The ARRC is not a waste-to-energy facility.

Waste-to-energy facilities use combustion or similar thermal processes to generate electricity or heat from waste. The ARRC does not burn waste. Its primary purpose is to recover materials so they can be reused in local industry.  

The ARRC is a resource recovery facility, where modern technology is used to:
	•	clean and prepare waste
	•	sort materials using AI and robotics
	•	separate organics and recyclables
	•	create useful feedstocks for local businesses.

The ARRC may use bioenergy to help power parts of the facility, but this is very different from waste-to-energy incineration.
 [For reactive purposes only] In rare cases, a small portion of low-value residual material may be sent to external waste-to-energy facilities, but only where there is no higher-value circular use available.

In short: the ARRC focuses on recovery first. It is designed to keep materials in the circular economy, not to incinerate waste.

You can learn more at www.advancedenergytech.com.au or contact [AET contact name] for further information.

7. Appendix 
7.1 Additional technical Q&As (For technical stakeholders, regulators & industry)

Facility Overview
Q. What is the purpose of the ARRC?
The ARRC is a modern, enclosed resource recovery facility designed to:
	•	recover valuable materials from household waste
	•	reduce landfill and environmental impact
	•	support local manufacturing and circular industries
	•	cut greenhouse gas emissions
	•	provide councils with predictable long-term pricing
It processes residual waste (red bin) and commingled recyclables (yellow bin), with capacity for selected commercial waste streams.

Q. How much waste can the facility process?
	•	Initial capacity: around 30,000 tonnes per year
	•	Modular expansion: scalable up to approximately 80,000 tonnes
	•	Modules can be added as councils join or population grows.

Technology & Process
Q. What is “precision sorting”?
Precision sorting combines:
	•	AI and computer vision – recognises materials by shape, colour and composition
	•	Robotics – removes and sorts materials quickly and accurately
	•	Optical/NIR sensors – identify materials by their chemical signature
	•	Air jets – direct items into correct streams
This allows high recovery from mixed waste, solving a major challenge for regional areas.

Q. How are organics recovered and used?
Separated organics can be used for:
	•	soil enhancement products
	•	composting
	•	bioenergy co-products
	•	potential agricultural applications
Recovering organics is a major source of emissions reduction.

Q. What recyclable materials will be recovered?
The facility will be able to recover:
	•	plastics (PET, HDPE, PP, LDPE and mixed plastics)
	•	aluminium and metals
	•	paper and cardboard
	•	glass fines
	•	mixed fibre streams
The process produces cleaner, more consistent feedstock for local industries.

Environmental Performance
Q. How will the facility reduce greenhouse gas emissions?
GHG reductions come from:
	•	removing organics from landfill (significant methane avoidance)
	•	reducing long-haul waste transport
	•	recovering materials with high embodied carbon
	•	enabling circular product manufacturing locally
Exact CO₂e savings will be quantified during detailed design.

Q. How are noise, odour and emissions managed?
The ARRC is a fully enclosed system with:
	•	negative air pressure
	•	odour treatment units
	•	modern filtration systems
	•	EPA-regulated emissions controls
These measures significantly limit off-site impacts.



Q. What about traffic impacts?
During construction, there will be some additional vehicle movements at certain times. These will be carefully planned and managed to minimise disruption, with a detailed traffic management plan guiding routes, timings and safety measures. If any works are expected to temporarily affect local roads, the community will be notified well in advance with clear information about what to expect and how long it will last.
Once the facility is operating, traffic impacts are expected to be modest and manageable. AET is planning to locate the ARRC close to major transport routes so that vehicle movements feed directly into the existing road network, minimising impacts on local streets and the broader community. 

Regulatory Requirements
Q. Which approvals will the ARRC require?
The project will operate under EPA Victoria’s updated permissions framework, which may include:
	•	a Development Licence
	•	an Operating Licence
	•	an Environmental Management Plan (EMP)
	•	mandated monitoring and reporting
AET will work closely with EPA Victoria through design, commissioning and operations to ensure all regulatory requirements are met. 
In addition, AET will work with the relevant planning authorities to ensure the ARRC complies with all planning and building regulations and is located within the appropriate planning zone.
Further details about planning pathways will be confirmed as part of the formal approvals process.

Circular Economy & Markets
Q. What circular economy outputs will the ARRC produce?
Recovered materials can be used for:
	•	plastics reprocessing
	•	soil and agricultural products
	•	construction materials
	•	biochar
	•	renewable manufacturing inputs
	•	innovation trials for new circular products


Q. How does the ARRC support local industry?
Local processing:
	•	keeps value in regional supply chains
	•	supports new manufacturing and reprocessing pathways
	•	reduces reliance on volatile global recycling markets
Businesses benefit from consistent, high-quality feedstocks.

Regional Benefits
Q. What are the economic benefits?
	•	up to 250 construction jobs
	•	up to 150 ongoing skilled jobs
	•	local procurement opportunities across trades, engineering and logistics
	•	attraction of new private investment
	•	creation of circular industries

Q. What are the environmental benefits?
	•	major landfill diversion
	•	significant GHG reductions
	•	improved soil and bioenergy pathways
	•	reduced truck movements
	•	cleaner local environment

Additional technical Q&As for Officers, Regulators & Industry
Q. What recovery rates are expected?
Final rates will be confirmed after detailed design, but international benchmarks show high recovery is achievable from mixed waste streams using this technology.
Q. How does the facility handle contamination?
Cleaning and pre-treatment processes along with precision sorting, significantly reduces contamination, enabling material recovery even from mixed regional waste.
Q. Will the facility cause odour?
No. Waste is cleaned early, processing is enclosed, and odour treatment systems meet EPA standards.
Q. What happens to hazardous materials?
Household hazardous items are screened out during front-end processing. Industrial hazardous waste will not be accepted.
Q. How is this different from waste-to-energy?
Materials are cleaned, sorted and reused. This is resource recovery, not waste-to-energy.
